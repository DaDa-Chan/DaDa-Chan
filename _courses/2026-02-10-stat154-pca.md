## 1. Warm-up: A Unique Principal Direction After Centering

PCA is a method of **dimension reduction**. The idea is to project a cloud of points $x_1,\dots,x_n\in\R^d$ (think $d\gg 1$) onto a lower-dimensional subspace that retains as much information as possible.

### 1.1 Energy and Central Tendency

Define the **energy** (or *inertia*/*variance*) of the cloud as

$$\mathcal{E}(x_1^n) \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i}^2.$$

The *central tendency* $m$ minimises $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i - u}^2$, which is achieved by the sample mean $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i$. This gives a **bias–variance decomposition**:

$$\boxed{\mathcal{E}(x_1^n) \;=\; \norm{\bar{x}}^2 \;+\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i - \bar{x}}^2.}$$

### 1.2 Centering and the Actual Variance

Define the **centered cloud** $\tilde{x}_i = x_i - \bar{x}$. Henceforth we assume centering has been performed as a preprocessing step, so $\bar{x}=0$. Under this convention, $\mathcal{E}(x_1^n) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i}^2$ is the *actual variance* of the cloud.

### 1.3 Axis Decomposition

Let $(e_1,\dots,e_d)$ denote the canonical basis of $\R^d$. Then the total variance decomposes along the coordinate axes:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i}^2 \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{d}\underbrace{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ip{e_j}{x_i}^2}_{\text{variance along }e_j}.$$

### 1.4 Beyond the Canonical Basis

What if no canonical direction explains significantly more variance? We should look for a more relevant direction—any unit vector $v\in \Sph$ (i.e.\ $\norm{v}=1$). The variance in direction $v$ is

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ip{v}{x_i}^2 \;\leq\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i}^2,$$

where the inequality follows from Cauchy–Schwarz. The **core idea of PCA** is to find $v\in\Sph$ that closes this gap as much as possible:

$$\boxed{v_1 \;=\; \operatorname*{argmax}_{v\in\Sph}\;\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ip{v}{x_i}^2.}$$

---

## 2. Solution via the Empirical Covariance Matrix

### 2.1 Quadratic Form Formulation

Recall that $\ip{v}{x}^2 = v^\top x\,x^\top v = v^\top(xx^\top)v$. Therefore

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ip{v}{x_i}^2 \;=\; v^\top\!\Bigl(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i x_i^\top\Bigr)v \;=\; v^\top \hSigma\, v,$$

where

$$\hSigma \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i x_i^\top \;\in\;\R^{d\times d}$$

is the **empirical covariance matrix** (recall $\bar{x}=0$).

<div class="note-box note-prop">
<span class="note-label">Proposition 2.1 — First principal direction</span>

The solution to $\max_{v\in\Sph} v^\top\hSigma\,v$ is the unit eigenvector of $\hSigma$ associated with its **largest eigenvalue**. This vector $v_1$ is the *first principal direction*, and the cloud has maximal spread along $v_1$.
</div>

<div class="note-proof">
<em>Proof.</em> Let $\lambda_1\geq\lambda_2\geq\cdots\geq\lambda_d\geq 0$ be the eigenvalues of $\hSigma$ (symmetric PSD) and $(u_1,\dots,u_d)$ a corresponding orthonormal eigenbasis.

**Method 1 (Lagrange multipliers).** At any constrained extremum $v^{\ast}$ there exists $\eta\in\R$ such that $2\,\hSigma\,v^{\ast} = 2\eta\,v^{\ast}$, i.e.\ $v^{\ast}$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\eta$. The objective at such a point is $\eta$. To maximise, choose $\eta = \lambda_1$ and $v^{\ast} = u_1$.

**Method 2 (direct spectral argument).** Expand $v = \sum_{j=1}^{d}\alpha_j u_j$ with $\sum_j\alpha_j^2=1$. Then

$$v^\top\hSigma\,v = \sum_{j=1}^{d}\alpha_j^2\,\lambda_j \leq \lambda_1\sum_{j=1}^{d}\alpha_j^2 = \lambda_1,$$

with equality iff $\alpha_1=\pm 1$ and $\alpha_j=0$ for $j\geq 2$, i.e.\ $v=\pm u_1$. $\square$
</div>

### 2.2 The Second Direction, Formally

Given $v_1 = u_1$, the second principal direction solves $\max_{v\in\Sph,\,\ip{v}{v_1}=0} v^\top\hSigma\,v$. Expanding $v = \sum_j \alpha_j u_j$, the constraint $\ip{v}{u_1}=0$ forces $\alpha_1=0$, so

$$v^\top\hSigma\,v = \sum_{j=2}^{d}\alpha_j^2\lambda_j \leq \lambda_2,$$

with equality at $v = u_2$. By induction, the $k$-th principal direction is $u_k$ with explained variance $\lambda_k$.

## 3. Iteration: Multiple Principal Components

### 3.1 The Second Principal Direction

Having found $v_1$, we seek the second most important direction $v_2$ orthogonal to $v_1$:

$$v_2 \;=\; \operatorname*{argmax}_{\substack{v\in\Sph \\ \ip{v}{v_1}=0}} \;\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ip{v}{x_i}^2.$$

### 3.2 Joint Formulation

The first two principal directions can be found jointly:

$$(v_1,v_2) \;=\; \operatorname*{argmax}_{\substack{u,v\in\Sph \\ \ip{u}{v}=0}} \;\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\bigl[\ip{v}{x_i}^2 + \ip{u}{x_i}^2\bigr].$$

The key observation: $\ip{v}{x_i}^2+\ip{u}{x_i}^2$ equals the squared norm of the **projection** of $x_i$ onto $\operatorname{Span}\{v,u\}$.

### 3.3 $k$-Principal Directions as a Subspace Problem

This generalises naturally. The $k$-principal directions determine the $k$-dimensional subspace $F\subset\R^d$ with maximal *explained variance*:

$$\boxed{\max_{\substack{F\subset\R^d \\ \dim(F)=k}}\;\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{P_F(x_i)}^2,}$$

where $P_F(x_i)$ denotes the orthogonal projection of $x_i$ onto $F$.

<div class="note-box note-rem">
<span class="note-label">Remark 3.1 — Equivalent minimisation formulation</span>

By the Pythagorean theorem, $\norm{x_i}^2 = \norm{P_F(x_i)}^2 + \norm{x_i - P_F(x_i)}^2$, so maximising the explained variance is equivalent to **minimising the residual variance**:

$$\min_{\substack{F\subset\R^d \\ \dim(F)=k}}\;\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i - P_F(x_i)}^2.$$
</div>

## 4. Matrix Formulation

### 4.1 The Projection in Matrix Terms

Given a subspace $F\subset\R^d$ with $\dim(F)=k$ and orthonormal basis $(v_1,\dots,v_k)$, for any $x\in\R^d$:

$$P_F(x) \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{k}\ip{v_j}{x}\,v_j \;=\; VV^\top x,$$

where $V = (v_1\mid\cdots\mid v_k)\in\R^{d\times k}$ satisfies $V^\top V = I_k$. Then $\norm{P_F(x)}^2 = \norm{V^\top x}^2$.

### 4.2 PCA as Optimisation over Orthogonal Matrices

In terms of $V$, PCA becomes

$$\boxed{\max_{\substack{V\in\R^{d\times k} \\ V^\top V = I_k}}\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{V^\top x_i}^2,}$$

which is an optimisation problem over the **Stiefel manifold** (the set of $d\times k$ matrices with orthonormal columns).

<div class="note-box note-prop">
<span class="note-label">Proposition 4.1 — Solution via eigendecomposition</span>

The optimal $V$ is formed by the $k$ eigenvectors of $\hSigma$ corresponding to its $k$ largest eigenvalues $\lambda_1\geq\lambda_2\geq\cdots\geq\lambda_k$.
</div>

<div class="note-proof">
<em>Proof.</em> Rewrite the objective: $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{V^\top x_i}^2 = \operatorname{tr}(V^\top\hSigma\,V)$. Let $\hSigma = U\Lambda U^\top$ be the eigendecomposition. Substitute $W = U^\top V$ (which preserves $W^\top W = I_k$). The objective becomes $\operatorname{tr}(W^\top\Lambda\,W) = \sum_{j=1}^{k}\sum_{\ell=1}^{d}\lambda_\ell\,w_{\ell j}^2$.

Write $\beta_\ell = \sum_{j=1}^{k}w_{\ell j}^2$. Since $W^\top W = I_k$, we have $0\leq\beta_\ell\leq 1$ and $\sum_\ell\beta_\ell = k$. The linear program $\max\sum_\ell\lambda_\ell\beta_\ell$ subject to $\beta_\ell\in[0,1]$, $\sum_\ell\beta_\ell=k$ is maximised at $\beta_1=\cdots=\beta_k=1$, $\beta_{k+1}=\cdots=\beta_d=0$. This corresponds to $V = (u_1\mid\cdots\mid u_k)$. $\square$
</div>

## 5. Proportion of Variance Explained

Let $\lambda_1\geq\lambda_2\geq\cdots\geq\lambda_d\geq 0$ denote the eigenvalues of $\hSigma$, with eigenvectors $v_1,\dots,v_d$.

<div class="note-box note-def">
<span class="note-label">Definition 5.1 — Variance quantities</span>

1. **Total Variance (TV):** $\mathrm{TV} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i}^2 = \operatorname{tr}(\hSigma) = \sum_{j=1}^{d}\lambda_j$.

2. **Variance explained by the $j$-th PC:** $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ip{v_j}{x_i}^2 = v_j^\top\hSigma\,v_j = \lambda_j$.

3. **Proportion of Variance Explained (PVE):**

$$\mathrm{PVE}_j \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}\ip{v_j}{x_i}^2}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n}\norm{x_i}^2} \;=\; \frac{\lambda_j}{\displaystyle\sum_{\ell=1}^{d}\lambda_\ell}.$$

4. **Cumulative PVE by the first $k$ PCs:**

$$\mathrm{CPVE}_k \;=\; \sum_{j=1}^{k}\mathrm{PVE}_j \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{k}\lambda_j}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{d}\lambda_j}.$$
</div>

<div class="note-box note-rem">
<span class="note-label">Remark 5.1 — Choosing $k$</span>

In practice, plot $\mathrm{CPVE}_k$ as a function of $k$ (the *scree plot*) and choose $k$ at the "elbow"—the point beyond which adding more components yields diminishing returns in explained variance.
</div>
